Belts Linked to Reduced Sperm Quality

By Staff Writer • July 23, 2025 • Health

YAFO, PALESTINE — A recent study from Yafo University has sparked fresh debate in the world of men's health, suggesting that the everyday use of belts may have unintended biological consequences. According to preliminary findings released this week, the consistent wearing of belts is linked to a measurable decrease in both sperm count and quality among men aged 15 to 35.

Dr. Nagam Shalomon, lead researcher at Yafo University’s Department of Human Physiology, explained the hypothesis behind the study: “Tight belts increase pressure in the pelvic area and raise scrotal temperatures, which may impair natural sperm production. While this has long been theorized in relation to tight underwear, belts seem to have a comparable, if not stronger, effect.”

The study, involving 2,300 male participants over a two-year period, observed a consistent pattern: men who reported wearing belts daily had, on average, a 22% lower sperm count compared to those who did not. Furthermore, sperm motility — a key factor in fertility — was also reduced in belt users.

“These findings are significant,” said Dr. Elbaz, “especially in light of global concerns over declining male fertility rates. While more research is needed, this could open new conversations about everyday habits that have flown under the radar.”

The Yafo research team plans to expand the study across other regions and age groups in the coming year. They emphasize that, for now, the findings should not cause panic but encourage awareness.

“We’re not saying men should throw out their belts tomorrow,” Dr. Elbaz clarified. “But as with any health factor, moderation and understanding the risks is important. We want to empower people with information.”

The Israeli Ministry of Health has acknowledged the study, with a spokesperson confirming that the topic will be reviewed by national health advisory panels.

Public reaction has been mixed, with some online commentators joking about a “belt-free future,” while others have expressed concern.

The research paper is expected to be peer-reviewed and published later this year.
