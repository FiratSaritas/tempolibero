Central Texas Floods Claim Over 130 Lives

By Libertasio Staff • July 16, 2025 • Disaster

CENTRAL TEXAS — In early July 2025, Central Texas, particularly the Hill Country, experienced one of the deadliest inland floods in U.S. history. From July 4 to 7, a mesoscale convective vortex carrying tropical moisture from Tropical Storm Barry and Pacific remnants dumped up to 20.33 inches of rain in some areas.

Torrential rains caused the Guadalupe River to rise dramatically—nearly 29 feet in under an hour in parts of Hunt and Kerrville. Flash flood emergencies were issued for several counties, including Kerr and Mason.

The official death toll has surpassed 134, with at least 107 fatalities in Kerr County alone, and over 101 people remain missing. Hundreds were rescued, including dozens of campers at Camp Mystic near Hunt where more than 20 children were initially reported missing.

State and federal emergency responses mobilised more than 2,000 volunteers and multiple rescue teams, including U.S. Coast Guard swimmers who reached over 850 people.

The event has been compared to the Big Thompson River disaster of 1976, marking this as the deadliest inland flash flood in nearly 50 years.

Texas Governor Greg Abbott declared disaster zones for several counties, and President Trump signed a federal declaration for Kerr County. Authorities caution that continued heavy rain and flash flood watches remain in effect.

With significant gaps in early warning systems and the removal of Camp Mystic from federal flood maps in recent years, officials are now facing intense scrutiny over preparedness and land-use decisions.

As search and recovery continue, the scale of this disaster serves as a sharp reminder of changing weather patterns intensified by tropical remnants and raises urgent questions about infrastructure investment and climate adaptation.
